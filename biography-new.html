[a.l.s.lehodey]I've always believed there is something special about the stars. You can stare and stare and stare; they'll stare right back at you. There they lay, glimmering in the immensity of the night sky, having witnessed the entire drama of humankind, all three-hundred thousand years, as if it were but yesterday. The stars are a constant. The stars give us life. The stars are our friends.

And yet there's another striking thing about the stars: they're of the same substance as us, homo sapiens. Fine, not exactly the same substance. But close enough, for they too are bundles of atoms and hydrogen and helium with a little carbon mixed in. There's something very special about this: it serves as a reminder that we are of the universe; that consciousness occurs through us; and that reality is more than just our little egos. It reminds us there is beauty, there is love, and there is wisdom in this world. My story, as does that of humankind, starts with the stars. But for fear of becoming a little too abstract, our story shall start a little further down the line.

where it all started

It started in Reigate. Or more accurately, Redhill, a not-so-little town about twenty-miles as the crow flies from central London. To my American friends, I would describe Reigate as being to London what Connecticut is to New York. Suburbia, sure, but not quite the endless suburbia that one might find outside Houston or Los Angeles or Cape Town.

All those who know Reigate, as the Parliamentary constituency is named, would know that its two largest towns include the eponymous Reigate, and its less-nice neighbour Redhill. That statement would no-doubt spark controversy were it to be uttered in the town itself, but everyone, including myself knows it to be true. Reigate is quaint little English storefronts and centuries old relics, the town even mentioned in the Domesday book of 1086. Redhill is… Reigate's slightly more downtrodden neighbour, lacking a unifying architecture and tormented by anti-social behaviour.

And Redhill, was also,

home.

In an all-too-English tale, class did mean something to me, and motivated I was to do better. But time has passed. I have seen a lot more of the world and read a lot more than I had when I was younger. And I recognise that the British obsession with class is quite precisely that: a distinctly British obsession. That, perhaps, is what distinguishes the United Kingdom, and to some-extent ties it together. (A friend of mine once asked me: "Adam, what exactly is it that unifies Britain?" I was at a loss for a response. "The rule of law?", I spat out, no-doubt having internalised that response in-part from reading Hayek and in-part from the PREVENT lessons that had been drilled into us as seventeen year-olds). I asked myself, that first summer that I moved to the United States, what exactly is it that's so appealing about the British conception of class? Still further I thought about it, majoring (at SciencesPo) in sociology and economics, reading Bourdieu and whatnot, and concluding that enlightenment ought to be elevated over custom. Don't overanalyse for now; entire volumes could be written on this subject. One day I intend to, and the time for that will come.

There I was raised; there I was formed. In a bilingual Franco-British household, with parents who loved me dearly and did – as almost every parent does – what they believe to be the best for you. Another provocative claim: today the calculus of nations dominates. I, too, used to think using that logic: "I am," I told myself, "Franco-British." (Again, don't read too much into the ordering of those words. Were I to be pressed, I consider myself equally British and French, the order above one of linguistic custom). And yet, and yet, you move around a little and pause to think about it and you realise that nations are, at heart, "imagined communities," to use Benedict Anderson's phrase. Those cultures formed me, to be sure. But so too is each of us is heir to a global heritage. London influenced by Moscow, Moscow by New York, New York by Osaka, Osaka by Paris, and so on, and so on. Cities – their cultures and their economies included – continually "pass the torch onto the next," Jane Jacobs beautifully described in Cities and the Wealth of Nations.

It was around the age of seventeen that I really began to flourish into the individual I am now. That was the age at which I took – at risk of sounding conceited – what was mere academic excellence, and turned it into quite something else. I did that by reading a lot. A book a week, sometimes two books a week. I would peruse the shelves of my local library and borrow five books at a time, picking them out from the economics and politics sections and studying them closely after school. It helps that the British sixth-form system, whilst fairly rigorous, left me a lot of time to do so. There was no requirement to be in school when we didn't have lessons scheduled, allowing me plenty of time to read, learn to code, apply for internships, and whatnot. Sometimes I'd skip class and go to London where I'd sit in on lectures at the London School of Economics. Lunchtimes were usually dominated by political debates, at the time on the subject of Britain's exit from the European Union, with friends. And it was those same friends that I have to thank for encouraging me to set my sights on higher things.

And so it was that in the summer between Upper and Lower Sixth Form, the summer when I was seventeen, I kicked-off the professional side of my life after being selected for a prestigious sixth-form internship program at J.P. Morgan. Before I started, I had a vision, lying there on my bed, in good old Redhill. That vision was, in hindsight, so simplistic, but looked something like this: I would go to J.P. Morgan, ace that internship, land a return offer the next summer (which indeed I did), get into a top university, land the J.P. Morgan Spring Week offer, return the next summer for yet another internship, then make bank as an investment banker. It all looked so easy, it all seemed so sure. And… how glad I am that it never quite worked out.

The scene is one year later. I'd finished my A-Levels, and the "grand vision" – let us retrospectively call it that – was already in motion. I'd spent the summer galivanting round Europe, from Normandie to Paris to Barcelona, before returning for another stint at J.P. Morgan, after which I had decided it was time to get serious about the Spanish I'd spent two years perfecting during my A-Levels, which brought me to a small town midway between Pontevedra and Santiago de Compostela in the Galician region of northwest Spain. There I found myself, in a tiny little village where nadie hablaba inglés (not a soul spoke English!), thinking to myself "ouch I was less good at Spanish than I thought" and with ample time on my hands, considering I didn't have a smartphone (conscious choice) and all I had to do was read. Really, I had been drafted in to instruct a proud little Gallegan gentleman, then three years old, to speak English. As one does, as a proud little Gallegan child, he stubbornly refused and insisted we speak Gallego. Since I could not, we compromised, and I would speak to him in Castellano (standard Spanish). That I did, except his parents, who had brought me in, were also at home, leaving me with, as mentioned, plenty of time to think.

Come results day, into the inbox of my swanky little two-inch screened Nokia (conscious choice, I repeat!) slide my A-Level grades, and that thinking reached its apex as I puzzled over my future and considered whether I should take a risk and re-apply to Oxford. "Can I really, should I really, throw away an offer to UCL" – that top London university where I had secured a place – "for something that I'm not sure will even work out?" For one, my academic advisor was against it ("well you know I've seen some exceptionally smart people apply and not get in") and so too was dear Mother ("but you have a place at such a good school.") But I knew, I knew that I had to give it another shot, and that I'd regret it if I didn't. So reluctantly, I dialled UCL's number on my little Nokia, turned down their offer, and took a radically different path to that laid out in the "grand vision" I'd had lying on my bed one summer evening.

an American Dream

It might have started when my aunt and uncle came to visit one summer and started extolling the United States of America. Or maybe it was when I'd get home from school and tell myself I would watch one, singular episode of Sex and the City, only to be there three hours later. (And in the background, my father saying: "don't distract your brother, he's studying so hard!") It became more serious when, at the age of thirteen or fourteen, I'd scroll Google Streetview and plan out my future life commuting to work in New York City, working for none other than Google itself. (After all, at the age of thirteen, wouldn't you too want to work at a place with an actual slide in the office?) It always did remain a dream, at least at the back of my mind.

It remained a dream, until it didn't. There I was, taking another gap year – for a global pandemic was now underway. Fortunate I was, however, for after an ultimately unsuccessful second go at Oxford (such is life!), I had by then secured a role working in Parliament with plenty to do.

Except I had just returned from a weekend trip to Lisbon, where I revelled in what will, perhaps and hopefully for the only time in our lives, have been an actual speakeasy (given that the government had ordered bars be closed), after striking up conversation with the waitress at a Bairro Alto establishment who ushered me through a curtain into a lively basement party. Returned to London, the work had piled up and was only getting worse. My genius solution: get all the work done at night, when everyone would be sleeping and I could lock in distraction-free. For stimulation, I turned to podcasts. I shan't trace out exactly how I discovered the dual-degree program, though it was, indirectly, through one of these podcasts on the salacious life of one European politician that I came to learn of the SciencesPo-Columbia dual-degree program. From that moment on, I knew, just as I had before that time I first took the gap year, I knew that I had to apply. In French there is a phrase: "Mieux vaut des remords que des regrets." C'est vrai; it is true. I went all in, perfecting that admissions essay, sharpening my SAT skills, and doing everything in my power to make my application as competitive as possible. The future was still so indeterminate. Until everything fell into place that April morning when I awoke, from a Mexico City hotel room, to the news that I had been accepted on SciencesPo – Columbia University dual-BA program. That's the kind of news, that feels better than sex.
